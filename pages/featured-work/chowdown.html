ChowDown.

ChowDown is a food management app that helps users catalog their groceries, track pantry inventory, make more sustainable shopping choices, and discover meal suggestions to reduce food waste.

Started as my Master's Thesis, which was presented at the EDI Thesis Presentation '22 (starts at 1:54:09), it evolved into a full-fledged product that my co-founder and I eventually soft-launched to gather real-world feedback.

    User & Business Needs

    User: Better manage food in pantry to reduce food waste
    Business: Save more than just the environment

    Impact

    Acquired 400+ users and helped saved an annual estimate of $200K+ worth of food from going to waste.

    Contribution

    Self-initatied and soloed all design efforts from research to design system development

Problem

Managing food in the pantry is one of the many responsibilities of being an adult, but it is not an easy task. 40% of the entire US food supply gets thrown out every year and $161 billion worth of food goes down the drain. This is especially true among young adults who are living independently for the first time in their lives, because they lack the experience and knowledge for food management, which leads to food spoilage and waste.

Challenge: Young adults who are living independently for the first time often have trouble managing food in their pantry, because they lack the experience and knowledge for it, which leads to food spoilage and waste.

Research

I started off by conducting competitor analysis on existing solutions for reducing food waste, and found that there's an opportunity for a solution that is both inspiring and effortless to use. Then, I talked to young adults who often home cook and haven't been living independently for long about their experience with managing food.

    Competitor Analysis

    Analyzed 9 existing solutions for reducing food waste

    User Interview

    Conducted 30-45 minutes sessions with 14 young adults

Synthesize

From my research findings, I created a food journey map to show how young adults manage food from start to end, and then use it to identify the pain points of food management and potential opportunities for design. Here are the insights and HMW statements:

Journey Map

From the conversations I've had, I learned that young adults would buy food from grocery stores, store and preserve them for later uses, and finally they will either eat it or throw it out into the trash depending on the conditions of the food.

Simplified version of the food management journey.

Insights

    Awareness of Pantry

    Lack of awareness in their pantry makes young adults over-purchase food, which makes it difficult before they spoil

    Ambiguous Shelf Life

    Lack of clarity around shelf life of food prevents young adults from enjoying the process of using up food at their own pace

    Uncertainty of Food Usage

    Lack of direction in being resourceful with food prevents young adults from putting food into good use before they spoil

Design Challenge

How might support young adults in managing their pantry—from grocery shopping, to storing food properly, to using it up before it goes to waste?

Ideate & Test

With the design challenges, and the opportunity for an inspiring and effortless solution in mind, I brainstormed for potential ideas and developed the top 3 into high-level concepts to get feedback from participants.

High-level Concepts

Here were the 3 different directions that I played with:

    Grocery Split

    A grocery list and matching app that allows users to split groceries with other users

    Meal Inspiration

    A recipe app that checks for freshness of food and then show potential recipes

    Grocery Wallet

    A pantry list app in partnership with grocery stores that catalogs users' grocery items

Concept Testing

I tested the concepts with 7 young adults to hear their thoughts on the 3 concepts:

  • Users have concerns about the safeness and logistics of Grocery Split due to the nature of splitting with strangers
  • Users liked Meal Inspiration and Grocery Wallet, but Grocery Wallet came out on top because they like the lack of effort needed to catalog and keep track of their groceries

Getting feedback on Meal Inspiration from participants.

Design & Test II

After hearing feedback on the concepts, I decided to go forward with Grocery Wallet, but I also decided to incorporate the potential recipe feature from Meal Inspiration, because many users mentioned that they struggle to think of recipes on a daily basis. With the concept choice settled, I went on to designing the user flow and wireframes for ChowDown.

User Flow

I created a user flow to show how young adults can achieve their food management goals through my design by:

  • Cataloging their grocery items to keep track of their freshness
  • Finding potential recipes that will help use up food in their pantry
  • Using the app's shopping list to prevent over-purchasing food

Mid-fi wireframes showing the user flow of ChowDown.

ChowCard

The way ChowDown works is that users would go to one of the partnered stores to get their groceries, and, as they're checking out at the cash register, the cashier would scan a digital card on the app called ChowCard, which will automatically catalog their checked out items into the app.

How ChowDown and ChowCard works.

Pantry List

Users can view their cataloged items in the Pantry List and ChowDown’s algorithm will mark the items with expiration dates that the app will keep track of and notify if an item is expiring soon. Users can also tap on the pantry items to see information and advice on how to manage it.

Freshness notification and Pantry List.

Potential Recipes

Users can find recipes based on the ingredients that they have in their pantry list. The recipe page will tell users information like pantry match, ingredients and recipes from other channels that they can also refer to. After using up the ingredients to make the recipe, users can let ChowDown know and it will update their pantry list accordingly.

Potential Recipes and updating pantry list via recipe.

Smart Shopping List

Users can create a shopping list for groceries that will highlight items that you already have in your pantry. This way users can get groceries without worrying about over-purchasing food.

Shopping list highlighting items that are already in the pantry.

Usability Testing

I tested the design with 7 young adults and made 3 major improvements based on their feedback:

  • Changed how the pantry items are quantified
  • Added more information about a recipe's ingredients and the ability to adjust the amount needed based on serving size
  • Added a feature that allows users to update their pantry list by recipes that they've made

Conclusion

I really enjoyed working on ChowDown because finding ways to reduce food waste is a topic that I'm passionate about, and I was happy that my final design was positively received during the presentation. That being said, it was challenging to quantify the pantry items that makes sense to the user while it can also easily be updated in the pantry list. If I had more time, I hope to continue testing and iterating the way pantry items are quantified and make other improvements that are influenced by it.
