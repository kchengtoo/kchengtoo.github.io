Zima Mirror.

Zima Mirror is an interactive smart mirror designed to empower online shopping experience. It allows users to better visualize online clothes as virtual images in front of their reflection and easily cycle through different clothing using gesture control.

Problem

Online shopping is one of the most convenient ways for customers to purchase apparels, but the experience is not perfect. While forty-three percent of the purchases are influenced by online information, online sales are about nine percent of in-store sales. Without touching, fitting or feeling the clothes properly, many customers may end up buying the wrong size or quality, which leads to abandoned carts, order cancellations and returns.

Challenge: How might we enhance online shopping experiences in order to increase the success rate of online sales for apparels?

Researched and designed Zima Mirror over the course of thirteen months.

Research

In my thesis paper, I mentioned that one of the potential use cases for smart mirrors is online shopping, so I began by conducting interviews with participants who regularly shop online for apparels. I was able to learn their shopping behaviors, motivations to shop online and opinions on other online retailers, which allowed me to identify their needs as online shoppers.

    Framed user research based on thesis results

    User Interview

    Conducted 30 minutes interview with 4 participants

Synthesize

From the interview results, I was able to identify user pain points in online shopping and determine opportunities that this new smart mirror can take:

  • Hate dealing with returns and refuse to buy a product online if they cannot capture it mentally
  • "Gets off the site once they are distracted or bored," which shows that online shoppers have short attention spans

What they show vs. what it is.

Dull online shopping experience.

These pain points stemmed from the medium that is used for online shopping, because laptop and phone screens are not designed to run this kind of experience, but I believe that smart mirrors are. Smart mirror is a two-way mirror that has an electronic display behind the glass, which allows the device to project information on the surface of the mirror. Not only does this medium have an interface that can accurately display apparels, but also promotes new and engaging ways for users to interact with online retails.

Smart mirror as an appropriate medium for online shopping.

Design I & Test

I began with the foundation, and focused on designing and building the smart mirror. As the medium of the proposed solution, these were the two tasks that I needed to complete:

  • Created a blueprint for the smart mirror with the necessary components, core functionalities and measurements
  • Assembled the mirror with the listed components and materials

Smart Mirror Design

Zima Mirror has the basic functionalities that a standard smart mirror would have, but the most important addition was interactive capability. I designed the mirror to be controlled via gesture, because it enables engaging ways to interact with the mirror while keeping the surface smudge-free. So, I mapped out the components that are needed for the functionalities and how they are laid out on the mirror.

My sketches of the Zima Mirror's design.

Mirror Assembly

Out of the components and materials used for assembling the mirror, the mirror frame is the key component for housing all of the components together. I designed a thin-bezel frame that will maximize the space for displaying graphics and information on the two-way mirror. I also installed a USB hub that will allow a Leap Motion Controller to connect to the mirror and enable gesture based interaction.

Zima Mirror's assembly and components labeled. Back (L) and front (R).

Usability Testing

I exhibited the mirror at the Undergrad Open Studio 2019 to do some user testing and observed how the audiences interact with it. For the testing, I created a rough interface of a virtual dress room in which users can interact with virtual images of accessories.

My research supervisor, Brian O'Connell, interacting with the mirror.

After the participants tried out our smart mirror and rough interface, we were able to gather their feedback on the experience. The mirror caught many viewers' attentions at the exhibition, but it also raised a number of issues regarding the experience that I needed to address:

  • Implement more intuitive ways for users to recognize that the mirror is controlled via gestures
  • Create a more explicit cursor that sends feedback to the users when interacting with the virtual images

Design II

From the user testing, I learned that the user interaction of the smart mirror is not as intuitive as I thought, because most people are not familiar with this kind of technology. So, in the next iteration, I focused on designing the user experience of Zima Mirror and here's what I did:

  • Designed the interfaces for the online shopping experience
  • Came up with core gesture controls for interacting with the mirror
  • Designed a cursor that supports gesture-based interactions

Mirror Interface

I sketched out the interfaces that will contain the core functionalities for the mirror: Dashboard, Virtual Retail Store and Dress Room. An important note regarding the experience of the retail store and dress room is that it had to be suitable for the intended medium and familiar to users who are accustomed to in-store shopping.

Low-fidelity sketches of the smart mirror's interface.

So, for the Retail Store, I designed an interface that displays the virtual clothings in a horizontal carousel where users can shuffle through, which is similar to how clothes are displayed on hangers in real-life. As for the Dress Room, I refined the rough interface from the user testing by adding the ability to customize the color and size of the clothings

Hi-Fi Wireframes of Dashboard, Virtual Retail Store and Dress Room.

Gesture Review

I came up with gestures that users can perform with the mirror through the Leap Motion Controller that will define the online shopping experience and go well with the interface. Here were the four gestures that I settled on:

    Grab

    To move virtual clothings

    Tap

    To click buttons

    Swipe

    To cycle through the carousel

    Pinch

    Inwards and outwards to change the clothing's size

Cursor Design

In order to show how users' gestures are reflected on the mirror, I designed a white circular cursor with four smaller circles orbiting around it that mimics finger movements as the user's hand interacts with real-life objects. For example, when a user aims at a virtual clothing and clenches their hand into a fist, the cursor pulls in the four orbiting circles to its center and turns red to indicate that the cursor is performing the grabbing gesture.

Cursor for the smart mirror's interface.

Prototype

With all the design components completed, I integrated them and created a prototype that shows the Dashboard, Virtual Retail Store and Dress Room to help you understand how Zima Mirror works.

Conclusion

This project was the largest scale project that I've worked on. Since I created the mirror from scratch, I had to build a lot of components and conducted extensive research to understand the problem and its users. That being said, I enjoyed the journey of this project and I learned a lot about developing an end-to-end product. In the future, I would like to collaborate with someone who knows how to work with AR and apply it to the virtual dress room, so users can try out clothings from online stores through reflective AR.
